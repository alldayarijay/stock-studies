Equity Research & Scoring System

An end to end investment research system built on publicly available financial data covering ~1,200 US companies available on yFinance. Starting from raw data, it scores every company on business quality, relative valuation, and balance sheet strength, then surfaces the strongest investment candidates in a format designed for further analysis and financial modeling.

How It Works

The system is fully automated. One script pulls, cleans, scores, and exports everything. The pipeline runs quarterly and updates all ~1,200 companies in a single pass.

Step 1

Data Collection

Pull raw financial data from yFinance's API across all ~1,200 US companies. Income statement, balance sheet, cash flow, and market data.

Step 2

Data Pipeline

Python and SQL handle industry mapping, data quality checks, sanity bounds, and outlier flagging. Everything is cleaned before any analysis runs.

Step 3

Scoring Engine

Each company is ranked on quality and value metrics within its industry peer group. Scores are peer relative, not absolute, so a good score means "good compared to companies in the same business."

Step 4

Output to Excel

Results export to a formatted workbook with company scores, peer comparisons, visualizations, and worked scoring examples. Designed to surface the strongest candidates for deeper analysis.

Why this matters: Every quarter this system runs, it records a full snapshot of financial data across all ~1,200 companies. Over time, this builds a proprietary historical database that is typically only available through paid services: Koyfin ($39 to $79/mo), Financial Modeling Prep ($25 to $50/mo), or Wisesheets ($50/mo). Enterprise platforms like S&P CapitalIQ and Bloomberg cost thousands per year. This system replicates that data collection entirely for free and updates automatically every quarter with one script. After 12 quarters, the database will contain three full years of quarterly history for every company.

Scoring Methodology

49 metrics are pulled per company, but each company is scored on a maximum of four quality and four value metrics specific to its peer group. Using only four keeps the signal clean and the output easy to interpret. More metrics means more noise, and the goal is to highlight the handful of fundamentals that actually differentiate companies within each industry. The remaining metrics are retained as research context for deeper analysis. The three component scores combine into a single Quality Adjusted Value (QAV) score.

Quality Score

WEIGHT VARIES BY SECTOR

Ranks a company's fundamentals (margins, returns, growth) as a percentile against its peer group. Adjusted ±8 points based on 3 year trend direction, blending pipeline history with yFinance backfill. Requires a minimum of 3 peers with data per metric.

Value Score

WEIGHT VARIES BY SECTOR

Ranks valuation multiples vs. peers (lower is better). Negative multiples are automatically excluded. Mean reversion bonus of up to +10 points if the stock's P/E trades more than 15% below its own 3 year average. If less than 30% of value weight is available, the score defaults to 50 (neutral).

Balance Sheet Score

WEIGHT VARIES BY SECTOR

Interest coverage determines regime (Strong, Adequate, Tight, Stressed). Thresholds are industry adjusted: industries with contracted revenue and variable costs need less coverage cushion than those with volatile revenue and fixed costs.

QAV Score Composition (Technology Sector)

Quality 50%
Value 35%
BS 15%

Weights vary by sector. Energy emphasizes Value (45%), Utilities emphasize Balance Sheet (35%), Technology emphasizes Quality (50%). QAV is capped at 55 if Quality or Value falls below 30.

Each score carries a confidence level (high, medium, low) based on data completeness. Every score includes an audit trail: Data Gaps (what was missing or excluded and why) and Score Drivers (which specific metrics are driving the score up or down).

Limitations

Peer groups are imperfect. Scores are percentile ranks within industry peer groups. Some peer groups are too broad, meaning businesses with genuinely different models sit together because there are not enough public comparables to support a narrower grouping. Always look at who is in the peer group before acting on a score. Expanding to global coverage is the long term solution.

Scores are backwards looking. Every metric is historical. The market prices the future. A company can score poorly while the market has already priced in a recovery, and a company can score well because last year was exceptional while the business is quietly deteriorating. The score tells you where a company has been. The financial model is where the forward judgment happens.

Company Financial Models

Qualys (QLYS)

SaaS Cybersecurity · Technology

A cash generative SaaS business that has sustained 103% net revenue retention over the last three years. The market is pricing in the risk that platform consolidators like Palo Alto Networks and CrowdStrike bundle vulnerability management into their suites and drive QLYS's net retention below 100%. The model proves that if net retention holds at or above 100%, QLYS is a materially undervalued free cash flow compounder trading well below fair value.

Brinker International (EAT)

Casual Dining Restaurants · Consumer Discretionary

The Chili's turnaround has been one of the hottest restaurant stories of the last few years, driven by new CEO Kevin Hochman, a refreshed value strategy, and a potentially overhyped surge in brand relevance fueled by viral social media moments. Same store sales printed +17% in FY25A compared to a casual dining peer median of roughly 4%. The question is where comps normalize. The model tests how much of the current price is supported by steady state fundamentals vs. how much requires the hype cycle to continue.

Graco (GGG)

Industrial Machinery · Industrials

Graco is a high quality industrial compounder with 33% EBITDA margins, $600M of net cash, and a 100 year operating history. With organic growth running 1 to 3% through cycles, acquisitions are the real growth engine, which means M&A execution (paying reasonable multiples, integrating cleanly, not over-levering) is the single biggest swing factor. At ~$81 it trades in line with peers and slightly above DCF fair value, with the premium reflecting what a DCF mechanically undersells: moat durability and 89% capital return. The business is genuinely hard to break (diversified geographies, sticky distributor base, end-market mix). The real risk isn't demand destruction, it's overpaying today and earning only the steady mid single digit compound the business produces.

Capital One (COF)

Consumer Finance · Financials  ·  In Progress

Capital One closed its $35B acquisition of Discover in 2025, becoming the only US issuer that owns its own payment network alongside Visa, Mastercard, and American Express. The combined business is part bank (deposit funding, credit card lending, NIM driven) and part payments network (transaction fees, network economics). The framework debate is real: should COF be valued as a bank (P/E and P/Book on credit losses and NIM) or as a payments business (EV/EBITDA on transaction volume)? The model will test both lenses, separate Discover network synergies from the lending franchise, and frame whether the post deal stock is pricing in the network optionality or just the bigger credit card book.
